Why should I care?

During the pandemic, Singapore saw a massive spike in park users all over the nation. The lockdown proved to everyone worldwide that humans actually do rely on ecosystem services more than we thought. We must always keep in touch with nature, to de-stress or to have recreational fun, some plants and forests never hurt!

The Urban Heat Island Effect will increase in severity when we develop more urban areas without conserving adequate green spaces. Unmitigated development rates can lead to a higher risk of heat stroke and heat-related ailments.

We all rely on these forests and intertidal areas, whether we care to know or not. The first step to making a difference is to become "biod-aware". Become aware of the wildlife and habitats you see on your daily commute, or the different bird species that migrate through your street annually.

Success stories

Chek Jawa was slated for reclamation, but after long months of guided walks, petitions, and relentless outreach efforts and publicity, Chek Jawa's reclamation has been deferred.

Recently in the year 2026, the Housing Development Board, the developer of the Sembawang N5 plot, decided to scale back the size of the residential development to conserve about 6 hectares of its pre-existing secondary forest, due to its high value from its high ecological connectivity.

Our forests and reefs do not have a voice that can speak for themselves. We as continue doing our part as informed citizens, to ensure that our natural heritage is preserved for future generations. The government is always keen to listen to stakeholders, and we must keep pushing.

Our losses

As with all developed cities, we have lost plenty of our natural heritage. The only primary forests left on this island nation can be found near the summit of our Bukit Timah Nature Reserve, the Labrador Nature Reserve, and in the Nee Soon Swamp Forest. Other than these three places, we have lost all terrestrial forests to our country's centuries of human settlement.

Environmental Impact Assessments

Environmental Impact Assessments (EIAs) are done to discover the impacts to environmental receptors on the development site. EIAs are done when a developer wishes to build on a site that has been identified by the National Parks Board to have high enough ecological value. Learn more about EIAs here.
