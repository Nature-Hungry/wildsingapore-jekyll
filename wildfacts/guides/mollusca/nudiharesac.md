**Slugs:
nudibranch, sea hare or sap-sucking slug?  
How
to tell them apart?***updated
Jul 2020*

---

All these slugs
belong to Phylum [Mollusca](mollusca.htm) but come from
different Orders.   
They are often mistaken for one another.

In
most only the rhinophores are obvious, although in others, the oral
tentacles are also visible (they look like a moustache).
Usually
two pairs of large tentacles are visible, a pair of flappy oral tentacles,
and a pair of rhinophores.
Usually
only one pair of rhinophores visible.
![](slugs/nudibranchia/atromarginata/050529sjig7138m3b.jpg)
![](slugs/anaspidae/geographica/070614chgd2152m3.jpg)
![](slugs/sacoglossa/ornata/040423snt0116m3.jpg)
Some
have flowery gills on their backs. But these can be retracted into
the body. And some nudibranchs don't have these external gills. Some
nudibranchs also have a body made up of two flaps.
The
gills are enclosed in the body mantle. In many, the body is made up
of two flaps.
No
external gills. In many, the body is made up of two flaps.
Nudibranchs
have no external shells.
Some
sea hares have internal shells.
Some
sap-sucking slugs have external shells.
All
nudibranchs are carnivores. Most eat immobile animals such as sponges,
bryozoans and ascidians.
Sea
hares eat seaweed and tend to be seasonally abundant in synchrony
with blooms of their food source.
Sap-sucking
slugs eat seaweed too and pierce the cells to suck out the juices.
