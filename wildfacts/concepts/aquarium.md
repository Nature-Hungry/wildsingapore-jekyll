Marine aquariums
are a popular hobby. Irresponsible practice of this hobby, however,
damages the shores and reefs.  
  
Some facts (taken from Wabnitz, Colette, Michelle Taylor, Edmund Green
and Tries Razak, [From
Ocean to Aquarium: The global trade in marine ornamental species](http://www.unep-wcmc.org/resources/publications/UNEP_WCMC_bio_series/17),
UNEP World Conservation Monitoring Centre, 2003)

If done right,
some feel the aquarium trade can provide strong economic incentives
for coral reef conservation.

- Most marine
  life in the aquarium trade are NOT used for other purposes.
- Aquarium
  animals are the highest value-added product that can be harvested
  from a coral reef.
- If managed
  sustainably, the trade could provide jobs to low-income communities
  where there are few alternatives sources of income.

The aquarium
trade is big

- 1.5-2 million
  people worldwide are believed to engage in this hobby.
- The trade
  supporting the hobby is worth an estimated US$200-300 million
  a year.

Where do the
stock come from? Where does it go?

- Unlike freshwater
  aquaria where 90% of traded stock are farmed, most of marine aquarium
  stock are wild caught. Only 1-10% of marine ornamental fish are
  captive-bred and less than 1% of hard corals are from cultured
  origins.
- Southeast
  Asia is the source of animals for most of this trade.
- The main
  destination is the US, Europe and to a lesser extent, Japan.

What is traded?

- About 1,400
  species of fishes are traded worldwide, or about 20-24 million
  individual fishes annually.
  - The 10
    most traded species make up 36% of the trade.
  - Two species
    known NOT to do well in captivity nevertheless are in the
    top ten most traded species: these are the Cleaner wrasse
    (*Labroides dimidiatus*) and the mandarin fish (*Synchiropus
    splendidus*).
  - Damselfish
    (Family Pomacentridae) make up almost half of the trade.
- About 140
  species of hard corals are traded worldwide, or about 11-12 million
  pieces annually.
  - Species
    in 7 genera make up more than half of the live coral trade.
- About 61
  species of soft corals are traded worldwide, or about 390,000
  pieces annually.
  - Dendronephthya
    is one of the most commonly traded species but most usually
    die within weeks due to difficulty in replicating natural
    conditions in the aquarium.
- More than
  500 species of other marine creatures are traded worldwide, or
  about 9-10 million animals annually. Molluscs, shrimps and anemones
  make up most of these.
  - Cleaner
    shrimps and the large sea anemones (*Heteractis* sp.)
    account for 15% of invertebrates traded.
- 1-10% of
  fishes and less than 1% of corals are captive bred for sale. Thus
  almost all marine creatures sold in the aquarium trade are harvested
  from the wild.

Collection methods
damage the reefs:

- Live fish
  for the aquarium trade are often collected using cyanide or toxins.
  These kills off other reef inhabitants that cannot swim away.
  And the fishes collected in this manner often do not survive long.
- Some marine
  invertebrates are collected by destroying the dead or living corals
  where they hide.
- Overharvesting
  destroys a local population and can endanger the species.
- A large proportion
  of the animals collected die during shipping and processing.

Many marine
creatures cannot survive in captivity. Here are some of the reasons...

- Require special
  diets that are difficult to duplicate in captivity. Many such
  creatures simply die of starvation in a marine aquarium
- Require special
  habitats that are difficult to simulate. Such creatures die of
  stress in a marine aquarium.
- Dangerous
  to fish-keeper or other tank mates. These creatures may kill off
  everything else in a marine aquarium.
- Grow too
  large to be kept in a home aquarium. These creatures eventually
  have to be killed when they grow up.

Poaching on
our shores and reefs for home or commercial aquariums continues
to take place. Here is a documented attempt to remove hard corals
from Labrador Beach. Such activities can seriously affect local
populations of marine life.  
  
Sustainable harvesting of reefs for the marine aquarium trade?  
  
Some conservationists suggest that if managed properly, the aquarium
trade could support long-term conservation and sustainable use of
reefs.

- Harvesting
  for the aquarium trade results in more monetary returns for smaller
  damage. 1 kg of aquarium fish is worth US$500, but as food, only
  $6 (Maldives, in 2000). 1 tonne of hard coral for the aquarium
  trade is worth US$7,000 but as construction material only US$60.
  Live rock exported for the aquarium trade is worth US$2 per kilo
  but less than US$0.2 per kilo as construction material (Palau).
- Thousands
  of poor people depend on the trade for a living.
